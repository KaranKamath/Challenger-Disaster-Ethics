Existing Approaches

Literature Review

  • Quinlan (1986) – Seminal paper on the ID3 decision tree learning algorithm
  • Bisani (2008) – Paper that introduces G2P algorithm (that was released with the Sequitur tool) used in experiments. It achieved letter error rates of 8.62% and word error rates of 37.27% on the NETtalk 18k (English) datasets, beating earlier results on this dataset
  • Torkkola (1993) – Dynamically Expanding Context: Decision Tree approach that showed local context alone is not enough (and additional information about morphological structure and etymology is needed). Reported an accuracy of 91% on a 20,008 word English corpus.
  • Daelemans and van den Bosch (1996): Also used decision trees, but specify 2-step G2P alignment algorithm (for English). Based on the notion that similar words have similar spelling.
  • In Schlippe et al (2013): Web-based tools (Wiktionary and GlobalPhone), were leveraged to automatically create pronounciation dictionaries (using Sequitur, and showed that about 15k phone tokens were sufficient to train stable grapheme-to-phoneme models in 10 languages. Best word error rates of 20.97% were achieved for English.
  • In Veiga et al, a pronunciation dictionary for European Portuguese was created using a joint sequence g2p model. For grapheme to phoneme alignment, the 1-01 alignment was used, which allows cases where more than one grapheme originates a phoneme (deletion of phonemes). Training the dictionary on a pronunciation dictionary of 40k words yielded a word error rate of 2.44% and phoneme error rate of 0.32%.

Future Work

Alignment

Implementing better schemes to align grapheme units to phonemes (01 or m-to-n) is likely to lead to much better decision tree clustering performance (making relevant feature extraction more accurate).

Feature Set Expansion

For decision tree clustering, the feature set needs to be expanded to include morphological and etymological features, which are likely to lead to better performance.

Using Historical Links

For the P2P experiments (for Cantonese–Mandarin), historical links could be leveraged to analyze and find confusable mappings between the phonemes, and this knowledge could be used to encode more meaningful features when a decision tree approach is attempted for this problem.
