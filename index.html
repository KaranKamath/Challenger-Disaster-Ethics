Duty Ethics

Action #1

Morton–Thiokol

Managers violated moral duty to ensure the safety of the astronauts by clearing launch despite uncertainty about safety of O rings.

Engineers satisfied their duty to present their findings accurately and to try to convince management of the threat.

"Several engineers re-expressed their concerns about the effect of low temperatures on the resilience of the rubber O-rings that sealed the joints of the SRBs, and recommended a launch postponement." - Wikipedia

Action #1

NASA

Morton–Thiokol failed their duty to provide NASA with safe SRBs for the launch.

Managers violated their duty to give an accurate and honest analysis of the danger to NASA.

Even after finding the problem, they failed their duty to address it in a timely manner.

"They did not call for a halt to shuttle flights until the joints could be redesigned, but rather treated the problem as an acceptable flight risk" - Wikipedia

Action #2

Morton–Thiokol

NASA violated its duty as a contractee to listen to the concerns raised by Morton–Thiokol's managers, objectively. Instead, they intimidated them into clearing Challenger for launch.

During the conference call, Hardy told Thiokol, "I am appalled. I am appalled by your recommendation." - Wikipedia
Mulloy said, "My God, Thiokol, when do you want me to launch — next April?" - Wikipedia

Action #2

NASA

NASA violated its engineering duty to ensure safety of its shuttle and astronauts. They gave in to political pressure and let it compromise their judgement.

"President Reagan was scheduled to give the annual State of the Union address that very night, and, according to these critics, wanted to be able to tout the launch in that speech" - Wikipedia

"NASA’s budget was determined by Congress, which was becoming increasingly unhappy with delays in the shuttle project and shuttle performance. " - Fleddermann

Rights Ethics

Action #1

Morton–Thiokol

Morton–Thiokol satisfied their right to ensure business. They may have chosen to agree with NASA's decision instead of objecting to avoid losing NASA as a client.

Action #1

NASA

Morton–Thiokol violated NASA's right to an honest analysis on safety of the Challenger launch.

They violated the astronauts' right to stay safe by assuming that the problem was an "acceptable risk"

"They did not call for a halt to shuttle flights until the joints could be redesigned, but rather treated the problem as an acceptable flight risk" - Wikipedia

Action #2

Morton–Thiokol

NASA violated Morton–Thiokol's right to survive and thrive as a company by forcing them to clear Challenger's launch and risking the company's reputation.

Action #2

NASA

NASA satisfied their right to ensure the survival of the space shuttle program and stay ahead in the Space Race.

"NASA was feeling some urgency in the program because the European Space Agency was developing what seemed to be a cheaper alternative to the shuttle, which could potentially put the shuttle out of business" - Fleddermann

"NASA’s budget was determined by Congress, which was becoming increasingly unhappy with delays in the shuttle project and shuttle performance." - Fleddermann

Virtue Ethics

Action #1

Morton–Thiokol

Engineers violate virtue ethics by acquiescing irresponsibly to a decision under pressure from managers

Managers violate virtue ethics by giving up their moral considerations under pressure from NASA

Managers violate virtue ethics by being deceitful in the presentation of the threat perception

Action #1

NASA

Managers violate virtue ethics by irresponsibly risking the lives of the astronauts in their employ

Managers also violate virtue ethics by turning a blind eye to engineering concerns raised, focussing only on their loss of face in case of failure to launch

Engineers and Managers irresponsibly suggest secondary backup of O-Rings being present, which is in clear contravention of their own policy on Criticality 1 components

Action #2

NASA

NASA irresponsibly prioritizes public relations over Challenger safety

Dishonest in the manner in which they turn a blind eye to conditions of the launch, instead focussing on how it is "appalling" if it is postponed (despite MT and Rockwell expressing concerns)

Let their impatience trump reason

Action #2

Morton–Thiokol

Irresponsibly fail to point out NASA's flawed arguments for launch, despite having the data

Act Utilitarianism

Action #1

Morton–Thiokol

Act Utilitarianism is satisfied as a successful launch would preserve the business of the company as well as promote the company’s business. The benefits outweigh the costs which could include the negative publicity from a disaster.

Action #1

NASA

Act Utilitarianism is satisfied as a successful launch will bring in support for NASA programs and NASA had the recommendation of the company that made the component. It can also be argued it is not satisfied since Morton–Thiokol were previously hesitant and disaster would bring negative publicity for NASA

Action #2

Morton–Thiokol

Act Utilitarianism is satisfied as going against NASA would mean that the company could lose business. In addition, NASA was insistent that the launch go ahead after it had been postponed many times previously.

Action #2

NASA

Act Utilitarianism is satisfied as a successful launch in front of vice president would mean continued support for future NASA missions as well as support from the public on the insistence of VP.

Rule Utilitarianism

Action #1

Morton–Thiokol

If Morton–Thiokol does not have any rules on how to react on this kind of situation, then it is satisfied. Otherwise, it is not if they are rules that suggest a launch should not go ahead if it hasn’t been tested in prevalent conditions.

Action #1

NASA

Rule Utilitarianism is satisfied as rule state that the launch can go ahead if it is recommended by the engineers who developed the component.

Action #2

Morton–Thiokol

Depends on the Morton–Thiokol’s rules. If they state that the launch should not go ahead below certain temperature or if has not been tested at prevalent temperature, then rule utilitarianism is not satisfied. However, it there are no rules, then it is satisfied.

Action #2

NASA

It depends on the rules that NASA has. If NASA has rules that launch should not go ahead below a certain temperature, then rule utilitarianism is not satisfied. However, if there are no rules, then it is satisfied.
